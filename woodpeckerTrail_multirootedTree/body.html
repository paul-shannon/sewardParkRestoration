Site introduction

A multi-rooted bigleaf maple stands at a corner of the Woodpecker Trail. Its shape beguiles - children are drawn to it, and over the years it has hosted playing, running kids. In this it is like the Fairy Tree.

And like the Fairy Tree, this tree invites overuse - well-intentioned, and exuberant, but nonetheless harmful and destructive of forest health.

Bare ground in a Pacific Northwest forest is dead ground. As it compacts and expands from foot traffic, the fabric of the forest ecosystem grows weak.

A Collection of Sticks

On first encounter, and perhaps second, one sees here a playful collection of sticks artfully arranged on the maple tree. Another perspective, however, is worth considering. To gather wood from a fragile forest, no matter how small the sticks, no matter how pleasing the result, no matter how much we admire kids creativity, we think this is not the place. Here, in this already overused site, in an increasingly threatened forest, we - reluctantly - view this as yet another instance of disruption of and damage to the ecology of this site.

(Note that ten years ago the Friends of Seward Park, recognizing that kids connect to the forest through DOING, lobbied hard for a "rough use" set aside second growth forest nearby at Seward Park, where kids could play and jump, dig and build in the forest, We failed to persuade. Some of us still see this as a much needed complement to the Magnficent Foreast.)
