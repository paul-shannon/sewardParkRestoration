Research into the Ongoing Health of the Old-Growth Forest at Seward Park

This forest is 500 years old, largely intact here for five centuries. In a deeper sense, the forest is 8000 - 10,000 years old, continuous at this site since the end of the Ice Age, dynamically regenerating after stand-replacement fires which occur about every thousand years, thousands of species of plants, animals and fungi evolving together, some in competition, some in mutualistic collaboration.

Our goal is to foster the health of this forest for at least the next 500 years.

The accelerating effects of the Anthropocene pose many threats. Novel pathogens introduced, aggressive invasive plants taking over, human overuse, pollution, abnormal precipitation patterns, and rising temperatures. These all threaten the health of this previously resilient forest.

We humans are the indirect, and sometimes direct cause of these threats.

We can also be its protector. We can discourage overuse and restore bare ground. We can research and maybe sometimes mitigate and remedy disease effects. We can track its adaptations to climate change, understand the processes at work, manage with a light hand.

Seward Park's 120 acre old-growth forest is a rare remant of a million acres of the forest which for 10,000 years filled the lowlands surrounding the Salish Sea. These forests had been resilient against fire and insects, robust against floods and volcanoes, had been in part sustainably managed by indigenous people. The forests returned to good health and rich biodiversity after every disturbance.

That changed 170 years ago. With a hundred years, all but a thousand of those million acres were logged, cut down, and even mostly removed from memory.

But not at Seward. Here we see a classic lowland Douglas-Fir/Cedar/hemlock PNW forest, large enough to be a functioning ecosystem. It grew up after a stand-replacement fire 500 years ago, then partly survived a less severe fire 200 years later. About a 100 mature doug firs, with thick bark, a few cedars, and (we conjecture) many sword ferns were among the survivors. The forest is an old and beautiful place. Many firs are 6 feet in diameter and more. The forest floor includes, and is sometimes dominated by, ancient individual sword ferns, each possibly a thousand years old, as has been suggested to us by fern botanist David Barrington. Like the Douglas-Fir, sword ferns colonize open ground, rarely reproduce under a closed canopy, and live very long lives. Like in the fir, endophytic protective fungi plausibly provide defense for the fern against rapidly evolving pathogens and herbivores. Such mutualisms are apparently needed by all long-lived plant species. These mutualisms, easily seen (as in nurse logs and flower pollination) or hidden from view (as with the endophytes) are part of the beauty of this forest.

However, at present and for the last decade, all is not well in the forest. Two crucial species (sword fern and western hemlock) have severe and regionally unprecedented mortality. These may be transient effects: we have seen some signs of reduced mortality in the ferns. The hemlocks do not yet provide any reason for optimism.

As citizen scientists, we study the forest, monitor populations, run simple experiments, restore bare ground created by die-off and enthusiastic overuse by visitors and their dogs. We call this 'science by bake sale' or perhaps more accurately, self-funded shoestring operations abetted, now and again, by small amounts of donated funds, and excellent support from Seattle Parks

Our study of the fern die-off has produced substantial evidence for a water-borne pathogen, and against drought causes. We have some interesting hemlock data. Seattle Parks has done excellent work removing invasives, and supplying mulch and native plants for our restoration projects. We have a scientific advisory board (SAB), described below .

These studies and work projects will continue. However, some of the research question require more money and skill than we volunteers can provide. We need your help, researchers and funders alike.

To that end, we have prepared a summary of our past projects, and suggestions - with cost estimates - for possible new projects, on new topics, but also and primarily, on the problems we have already identified.

    Sword Ferns: Test the water-borne pathogen transmission hypothesis using functicide & antbiotics
    • Background: Along with Koch's postulates, this test is basic applied biochemistry. If the transmission effects disappear when the water has fungicide present, or antibiotic, then those broad classes of organisms (fungus or bacteria) are identified, simplifying subsequent work.
    • Benefits: validate and refine, or reject, the pathogen hypothesis prior to subsequent deep sequencing
    • Staffing: plant pathologist and assistant for three weeks
    • Infrastructure: green house bench space, beakers, assays
    Sword Ferns: Multi-dimensional analysis of an active die-off site vs control
    • Background: this is not hypothesis-driven science, but exploratory, with multiple assays. We have two large active die-off sites in the center of the forest, each with steady progression of effect and mortality, some 20% survival, and with healthy ferns nearby: well-posed "natural experiments".
    • Benefits: new hypotheses may arise, possibly indicating complex causality
    • Staffing: soil, insect, ecosystem and plant pathology scientists, working collaboratively, for 3 months
    • Infrastructure: a mix of field and lab work across several disciplines
    Western Hemlock: Multi-dimensional analysis of mortaility
    • Background: The fungus Rhizoctonia butinii is reported by OSU's LeBouldus Lab to cause needle loss like what we see. Is it indeed present at Seward? Is it found in the few apparently still healthy hemlocks, prior to defoliation? A few trees of different ages are as yet unaffected. Why? Would watering selected trees during drought reduce its compounding effects, contribute to tree survival? Note that widespread decline was observed a decade before the dramatic heat of 2015.
    • Benefits: R. butinii could be confirmed, disproven, or found to be but on of multiple agents.
    • Staffing: plant pathologist, two weeks
    • Infrastructure: field collection tools, laboratory culture, perhaps PCR or DNA-sequencing
    Forest Health: Ongoing dead/bare ground restoration projects
    • Background: areas of dead and bare ground appear regularly in the forest, due to off-trail human and canine trampling, or to sword fern die-off. These small sites are ecological deserts. Their proliferation weakens the forest as a whole, creating the possibility for further declines. Natural regeneration is very slow beneath a close canopy, so human assistance is worthwile. Our recent three-fold strategy shows promise, but needs long-term assessment and refinement: use only hardy native plant species, deep mulching a year in advance of planting, with subsequent monitoring of plant & soil health.
    • Benefits: long-term integrity of the forest
    • Staffing: volunteer groups to move mulch and plant, Seattle Parks/Green Seattle Partnership for mulch and plants, follow-up monitoring for five years, observational reports of survival (1 citizen scientist at quarterly intervals) recurring lab and technician studies, also at quarterly intervals
    • Infrastructure: inexpensive remote soil moisture sensors, environmental sequencing to determine relationships between soil biome and plant health.
    Forest Health: Census, proxied by assessing tree mortality, and that of other plants, at regular intervals.
    • Background: long-term forest monitoring is crucial to understanding and responding to forest problems. A lot of this work can be done by volunteers, managed by trained naturalists.
    • Benefits: Long-term forest health depends upon long-term forest monitoring.
    • Staffing: lay scientist or naturalist with good knowledge of the forest, data acquisition and analysis skills, part-time episodic work, into the far future. Currently - but not sustainably - we staff this with volunteers.
    • Infrastructure: ongoing modest salary support, volunteer engagement, long-term data storage. With increased staffing and training, far more complete forest health monitoring can be accomplished.
