Woodpecker Trail Stone Steps Area

The area around the stone steps on the Woodpecker Trail has been of concern for at least a decade. We did extensive restoration planting in 2015 in the area to the west - where now (again) you can see a trail leading over the hill and towards the south. This trail beckons - actually leads nowhere - but attracts a steady number of interested walkers.

This area was a favorite of the "nest builders" earlier in the decade, as was the east hill clearing just to the south.

Panorama

A panoramic survey. Note the "no boot" sign, installed in 2015. It has been, at best, only partially successful in discouraging off-trail travel.

In a Pacific Northwest early old-growth forest, bare ground - unless transient and quickly recovering - is dead ground. Off-trail travel creates and perpetuates dead ground.

Nurse Log, Interrupted

Fallen trees, left to decay over hundreds of years, play a vital role in the old growth ecosystem, rich in species diversity, essential to the life cycle of plants and animals. Pictured here is a doug fir broken up by human use - abuse - unable to play that role.

Unwelcome Art

Catherine Alexander photographed arrangements of sticks, debris and branches. One might interpret these, on first view, as benign.

But these efforts require off-trail travel to collect naturally occuring, naturally distributed coarse woody debris. They exemplify and encourage a view of the forest as a human creative playground, rather than as a fragile old-growth forest we wish to see grow on its own terms, and according to its own processes.
