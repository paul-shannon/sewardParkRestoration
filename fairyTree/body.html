Site introduction

In 2012, the Friends of Seward Park with strategic support from Michael Yadrick of Seattle Parks, and help from the staff and students from the Wilderness Awareness School, installed snow fencing and plants in pursuit of two goals:
  1. Revegetate the most impacted areas of this site, especially the hard-packed soil at the entrance from the Lost Lake Trail, and along the right low ridge extending from the doug fir at the entrance.
  2. Encourage ongoing use of the tree, especially for children.
The entrance area is now (2020, 8 years later) in pretty good shape. But the region beyond the fairy tree's upturned rootball, and the previous cedar tree understory to the right, are both heavily trampled and denuded.

A July 9th meeting between the Seattle Parks Natural Area Crew (NAC) - Chris Baines and Bridget Kelsh - plant ecologist Eric Sterner, and the Friends of Seward Park produced a plan:

  1. mulch the ground heavily (accomplished quickly by the NAC, July 2020)
  2. construct log and branch barriers to discourage use below the cedar tree and beyond (a certain distance beyond) the upturned rootball (pending).
  3. We discussed growing an obstacle fence, or perhaps devising a hybrid structure/live plant barrier, to replace the 2012 snow fencing and to protect beyond the root ball and below the cedar tree.

      Barrier Logs Installed, below the cedar, 3 sep 2020

      Chris Bain and his NAC crew brought in some big logs, and installed them as barriers to travel in two places:
      1. below the cedar tree
      2. about 15' behind the rootball.

      This is very fine work - we are really grateful to Chris, the NAC, and to Seattle Parks. I propose (this will be a good forest steward + volunteers job) to bring in large amounts and variety of coarse wood and branches to further deter travel into these areas, and to speed the recovery of the trampled & compacted ground - a good job for forest stewards and volunteers.
